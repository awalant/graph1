The United State of Sex Education

In AP US History, a course offered in all 50 of the states (plus the District of Columbia), the students all cover the same units with similar information at similar times. At the end of the year, all of the students who are taking the course sit for the exam on the exact same day. Then, at the beginning of the next school year, many of those students go off to their respective colleges and find themselves engaged in the current “epidemic” of sexual assaults on college campuses throughout the United States--they become bystanders, witnesses, perpetrators, and victims.

A little under a quarter of college-aged females have reported as experiencing sexual assault during college, and it is important to note that it is currently expected that only 5% of all sexual harassment cases on college campuses are actually reported. Students have an increased risk of experiencing sexual assault within their first few months on campus, as 50% of all reported sexual assaults occur between August and November.

In trying to rationalize why this might happen, it seems rational to start to explore the idea of what students might be learning about sexual health and wellness before they go away to college--what might be influencing them that makes these rates so high? Upon further investigation, it quickly becomes evident that unlike the treatment of AP US History, sexual health and wellness is a topic that is covered in varying capacities, if at all. There is no standard set for what students should be learning, no typical curricula to be followed, and increased mythology around topics like rape and oral sex that leads to misinformation. Studies cannot draw any actual conclusions about the impacts of sex education, both where it succeeds and where it fails, because students have an absurdly varied experience.

Let's explore the ways in which the states handle sex education differently, at least in its legal documentation.

*It is important to note that just because sex education may be mandated in a state, does not mean that the state is providing a comprehensive sex education for its pupils (but more on that later).

States with mandated sexual education

In the US, only 24 states (plus the District of Columbia) have it legally mandated for sex education to be taught in schools.

Alaska, Arkansas, Nebraska, South Dakota, and Wyoming currently have no laws or legislation regarding sexual health and wellness, so they have not been included.

The states in light pink are the states that call for it, and the states that are in dark purple currently do not.

States with medically accurate information

So far, only 13 states require that their provided education on the topics must be medically accurate–all the others do not require this.

Fun fact: this is as many as the original colonies of the United States in 1607-1776, and includes five states that do not mandate sex nor HIV education: Colorado, Florida, Louisiana, Texas, and Virginia

States where parents are allowed to remove their kids from the class

37 states, just over 80% of the states with relevant legislation, allow parents to remove their children from courses covering the topics of sex or HIV education.

Three states, Arizona, New York, and Pennsylvania allow students to be removed only from classes focused on HIV material.

Only seven states do not allow children to be pulled from these classes.
