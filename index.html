The United State of Sex Education

The history of sex education in the United States is a lot more progressive than one might imagine given the current status of it in schools throughout the nation. In the early years of establishing public school, sex education was taught in a fairly comprehensive matter. Throughout the times of war, the topic was covered in schools and basic training, mostly to guard against soldiers picking up sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) and prostitutes while overseas. After the war, the attitude towards sex education changed more towards family planning, and placed more of an emphasis on gender roles. This spurred a strong counter-culture reaction in the 60’s and 70’s that pushed individuals to discover themselves and their sexualities. It wasn’t until the 1980’s and the explosion of the AIDS crisis that the tone of sex education in the united states changed, and it was pushed towards a staunch and religious model of abstinent until marriage, also known as the abstinence-only education model.

Nowadays, sex education, unlike science, history, and even gym class, has no centralized standards that define the topics that schools must cover or the learning outcomes that students must exhibit in order to be deemed proficient. The topic of sexual health and wellness as a whole has been left to the determination of individual states, and in some states is left to the individual districts. The most comprehensive sex education laws at this point come from California, where they require the teaching of consent in schools, and mandate that schools must provide age appropriate, medically accurate, and culturally unbiased information on sex and HIV. Meanwhile, Alaska, Arkansas, Nebraska, South Dakota, and Wyoming currently have no state laws and leave it to their individual districts to decide what to teach in school. The rest of the states lie somewhere in between.

The various curricula implemented state to state, and even district to district, have varying effects on the students in those schools. The state laws, in combination with personal factors such as religious intervention or parental/sibling guidance, contribute towards the future experiences of these young, developing adults.

The experiences of these individuals differ because of the laws in their states, and whether or not others became involved with their sexual health and wellness education. It’s understandable that studies have had a hard time understanding the impact of sex education in the United States as a whole.

It is also understandable that then, once in a college setting, there has been an “epidemic” of sexual assault. Once on campus, students from all districts, states, and countries melt together, without parental guidance and with the influx of substances such as alcohol and drugs. The understanding of the topics that would be covered in a comprehensive sex education course vary widely from person to person, leading to different interpretations and implementations of the same concepts.

In a perfect world, all students (and all adults, for that matter) would be able to properly define sexual assault and consent so they may be more aware of when it is happening to them, or recognize when they are making someone uncomfortable.

Researchers looking into the risk factors associated with being both a victim and perpetrator of sexual assault attempted to ascertain a link between sex education and sexual assault. Their results in this particular arena were inconclusive, except to say that students who were taught "refusal skills," the ability to say no or negotiate for activities they are more comfortable with, were less likely to become victims of sexual assault on campus. Students who were in programs that taught more comprehensive sex education were more likely to receive this education.

While in this case the small preventative link between sex ed and sexual assault is great, the fact that the researchers were unable to find a more apparent connection is unsettling. They attributed this failure to find the impact of sex ed on the fact that the quality of sex education students recieved had varied so widely from student to student.
