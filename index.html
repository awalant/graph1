An introduction to American sex education

The uniform perception of sex education in the United States typically involves the cliché imagery of awkward, pimply teens and pre-teens spending their class time practicing putting condoms on bananas. [1] However, American education regarding sexual health and wellness is anything but uniform.

The standards around what is to be taught in school varies in every way, shifting in quality and guidelines from state to state, district to district, curricula to curricula. [2] These inconsistencies have a lasting impact on American youth, and result in detrimental long-term personal and public health effects. [3] In order to have a positive impact on the current standards (and lack-there-of), it is important to understand the current status of sex education in the United States.

The varying laws around sex education

Currently, sex education – unlike science, history, and even gym class – has no centralized standards that define the topics that schools must cover or the learning outcomes that students must exhibit in order to be deemed proficient. The topic of sexual health and wellness as a whole has been left to the determination of individual states, and in some states is left to the individual districts. [2]

The lack of unity in sex education has lasting impacts on children. The varying information, definitions, and amounts of shame given to children during these courses (if these courses are offered at all) can contribute towards different negative side effects: the repression of sexual and gender identity expression [5], the miscommunication in sexual encounters that can result in sexual assault [6], the misinformation that leads to positive pregnancy and STI test results [7].

Demonstrating the after effects of this educational system

To get a better understanding of the individual impact these factors have had on the thoughts and experiences of children nationwide, here are some select quotes from conversations with individuals ages 19–32: young enough to have taken sex ed recently, but old enough to be able to reflect on how it impacted them. They include current students graduating high school, recent graduates of high school and college, and educators. Additionally, those interviewed are people with an assortment of educational histories share their experiences, reflecting on how it impacted their sexual decisions and identities before, during, and after college.

"This year we were taught about the concept of consent in school, but we didn't go deep into it at all. And the reaction from most of the boys in my grade made me nauseous. People came up to me after class saying things like, "this is bullshit," like how one would react to a boring class or something." -current high school student

The experiences of these individuals differ because of the laws in their states, and whether or not others became involved with their sexual health and wellness education (such as parents, siblings, friends, or social media personalities). It’s understandable that studies have had a hard time understanding the impact of sex education in the United States as a whole. [8]

It is also understandable that then, once in a college setting, students are witnessing and experiencing an “epidemic” of sexual assault. [9] On campus, students from all districts, states, and countries melt together, without parental guidance and with the influx of substances such as alcohol and drugs. The understanding of the topics that would be covered in a comprehensive sex education course vary widely from person to person, leading to different interpretations and implementations of the same concepts. In practice, this can contribute towards miscommunications that result in sexual assault, albeit unintentionally. [10]

The result of the mixed messaging given to children has proven to be confusing at its best, and ineffective at its worst.

Researchers looking into the risk factors associated with being both a victim and perpetrator of sexual assault attempted to ascertain a link between sex education and sexual assault. [8] Their results in this particular arena were inconclusive, except to say that students who were taught "refusal skills" as part of a comprehensive curriculum had the ability to say no or negotiate for activities they were more comfortable with, and were less likely to become victims of sexual assault on campus.

While in this case the small preventative link between sex ed and sexual assault is a beneficial one, the researchers were unable to find a more apparent connection. They attributed this failure to find a quantifiable impact of sex ed on the quality of sex education that students recieved. The lessons learned varied so widely from student to student.

Improving sex ed

There are multiple levels at which change can occur.

At home

First and foremost, change has to occur in the home. [12] National surveys show that between educations received both at school and at home, only 16% of high school aged students reported having ever discussed sexual assault. [13] Education regarding consent, healthy relationships, and can start at any age, and it's important to have those lessons and discussions positively reinforced at home.

At school

Additionally, change has to occur at schools. It must first be established what kind of information is currently being taught in schools. [12] Even in states or districts where certain material is mandated, it does not actually ensure the topic is covered. These insights could be gleaned from a variety of sources, including but not limited to: current students, program supervisors or teachers, administrators, and at school board meetings. It is recommended that at a minimum, schools cover "anatomy and physiology, puberty and adolescent development, pregnancy and reproduction, sexually transmitted diseases and HIV, healthy relationships, personal safety." [12]

At the capitol

Finally, change must occur in the government. [12] By reaching out to local and state representatives, constituents can stress the importance of the material that should be covered in school, and why. Having laws in place that require content to be covered can help to protect teachers from parental litigation, and [in theory] means the schools have to comply with the regulations.

    States with mandated sexual education

    There are only 24 states, just under half of all US states, with mandated sexual health and wellness education. The remaining 26 states leave it to their local governments to decide on what should be taught.

    States where parents are not allowed to remove their kids from class

    Most states do not have rules that require students to take the class covering sexual health and wellness, even in 20 states where sex ed is mandated.

    States with medically accurate information

    Only 13 states require that the information taught in sex ed classes has to be medically accurate. Often, diagrams shown in textbooks used in health class omit the clitoris, and female pubic hair.
